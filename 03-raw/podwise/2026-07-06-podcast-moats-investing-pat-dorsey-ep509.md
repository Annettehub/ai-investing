Q \u0026 A\n\nQ: How did you develop the initial rubric for identifying businesses with competitive advantages?\n\u003e Q: 你是如何制定最初的标准来识别具有竞争优势的企业的？\n\nA: I started by looking at data back to the 1960s to identify every company that generated more than 15% returns on capital for over 15 years. This was a totally arbitrary threshold, but the goal was to move away from pure theory and let the data reveal patterns. I found that most of these companies could be traced back to specific buckets: intangible assets like brands or patents, high customer switching costs, network effects, or scale advantages. This became the foundational framework for analyzing competitive advantages, or \"moats,\" going forward.\n\u003e A: 我开始查看从 1960 年代以来的数据，以确定每个资本回报率超过 15% 的公司，持续超过 15 年。这是一个完全任意的阈值，但目标是摆脱纯理论，让数据揭示模式。我发现这些公司大多可以追溯到特定的类别：无形资产，如品牌或专利，高客户转换成本，网络效应或规模优势。这成为了分析竞争优势或 “护城河” 的基础框架。\n\nQ: Why do you now believe return on invested capital (ROIC) is less useful as a primary metric for identifying a moat?\n\u003e Q: 你现在为什么认为投资资本回报率（ROIC）作为识别护城河的主要指标不太有用？\n\nA: ROIC is less useful today because value creation has shifted away from capitalized assets toward expensed assets. Many modern software companies, for instance, generate high returns on capital simply because they don't have much capital on their balance sheets. When you build a brand or hire developers to write code, those costs are expensed rather than capitalized, even though the code base provides long-term value. Because of this, relying solely on ROIC can be misleading, and I find that qualitative analysis of a company's pricing power and ecosystem is far more revealing than a single quantitative ratio.\n\u003e A: ROIC 今天不太有用，因为价值创造已经从资本化资产转向了费用化资产。例如，许多现代软件公司之所以能产生高资本回报，仅仅是因为它们在资产负债表上的资本并不多。当你建立品牌或雇佣开发者编写代码时，这些成本是费用化的，而不是资本化的，即使代码库提供了长期价值。因此，仅依赖 ROIC 可能会导致误导，我发现公司定价能力和生态系统的定性分析远比单一的定量比率更具启发性。\n\nQ: How do you assess the quality of a management team, and what are the biggest red flags you look for?\n\u003e Q: 你如何评估管理团队的质量，以及你关注的最大警示信号是什么？\n\nA: My biggest goal is to look for humility, as it is easier to find teams that are unlikely to blow up than those that will do amazing things. I focus on avoiding \"left-tail\" risks—the catastrophic failures often caused by hubris. When I meet with management, I ask about their \"do-overs\"—what they would have done differently in the past—and how they speak about their team and board. If a CEO conflates themselves with the business, or if they are unwilling to listen to dissenting voices, those are major red flags. I also look for alignment; if a manager prioritizes their own comfort—like moving a headquarters to a sunny location at the expense of employees—it suggests they are not prioritizing shareholders or the business's long-term health.\n\u003e A: 我最大的目标是寻找谦逊，因为寻找不太可能崩溃的团队比寻找能做出惊人事情的团队更容易。我专注于避免 “左尾” 风险——由傲慢造成的灾难性失败。当我与管理层会面时，我会问他们的 “重来一遍”——他们过去会做出不同的决定，以及他们如何谈论自己的团队和董事会。如果一家公司的 CEO 将自己与业务混为一谈，或者他们不愿倾听持异议的声音，这些都是重大警示信号。我还会寻找一致性；如果一位管理者以自己的舒适为首要任务——例如以牺牲员工为代价将总部迁往阳光明媚的地方——这表明他们没有优先考虑股东或公司的长期健康。\n\nQ: Do you believe founder-run businesses are inherently superior to those run by professional managers?\n\u003e Q: 你认为创始人运营的企业天生就比职业经理人运营的企业更优秀吗？\n\nA: No, I don't think they are any better. We have a tendency, especially in Silicon Valley, to put founders on a pedestal, but the skill set required to start a company is very different from the skill set required to manage a 5,000-employee organization. While some founders, like Mark Zuckerberg, successfully evolve into good managers, many others do not. You have to interrogate them on a level playing field. I’ve seen plenty of founders who do dumb things because they won the lottery early on and stopped listening to anyone. You shouldn't give someone the benefit of the doubt just because they are a founder.\n\u003e A: 不，我并不认为它们更好。我们倾向于，特别是在硅谷，把创始人捧上神坛，但创办公司的技能与管理一个 5000 名员工的组织所需的技能非常不同。虽然一些创始人，如马克·扎克伯格，成功演变为优秀的管理者，但许多创始人并没有。你必须以公正的条件质疑他们。我见过许多创始人做愚蠢的事情，因为他们早早赢得了彩票，然后停止倾听任何人。仅仅因为他们是创始人，你不应该给予他们信任。\n\nQ: How do you evaluate the capital allocation skills of a CEO, given that most haven't had to do it before reaching the top?\n\u003e Q: 鉴于大多数 CEO 在达到高管之前并没有进行资本配置的经验，你如何评估他们的资本配置能力？\n\nA: Most executives rise through the ranks by being good politicians or functional managers, not by allocating capital. When they finally land in the CEO seat, they are suddenly expected to be experts at M\u0026A and buybacks, which is a learned skill. I approach their capital allocation with skepticism. I look at their M\u0026A history: did the deals make sense strategically, and what were their hurdle rates? If a company claims their goal is to \"beat the cost of capital by year three,\" that is a very low bar. I look for companies that are disciplined, that don't buy back stock at the wrong time, and that have an obvious, high-return internal reinvestment opportunity.\n\u003e A: 大多数高管是通过成为优秀的政治家或职能经理而晋升，而不是通过资本配置。当他们最终坐上 CEO 的位置时，他们突然被期望成为并购和回购的专家，而这是一项学习技能。我对他们的资本配置持怀疑态度。我会查看他们的并购历史：交易在战略上是否合理，对他们的门槛利率是什么？如果一家公司声称他们的目标是 “在第三年超过资本成本”，那是一个非常低的标准。我寻找那些有纪律的公司，它们不会在错误的时间回购股票，并且拥有显而易见的、高回报的内部再投资机会。\n\nQ: What are the primary benefits and risks of investing in non-U.S. companies?\n\u003e Q: 投资非美国公司的主要好处和风险是什么？\n\nA: U.S. companies offer two distinct advantages: the SEC, which is the most rigorous regulator in the world, and a higher standard of corporate disclosure. When you invest in a company listed in the U.S., you have a higher level of confidence that there isn't \"off-balance sheet hooey\" going on. Outside the U.S., you often see dividend and buyback policies that don't make economic sense, and corporate talent often migrates to the U.S. because the pay is significantly higher. That said, I keep an open aperture because there are global monopolies, like ASML, that are not listed in the U.S. but offer great value. You just have to be careful not to be the \"patsy at the table\" when it comes to local regulations or cultural nuances you don't fully understand.\n\u003e A: 美国公司提供两个明显的优势：证券交易委员会（SEC），这是世界上最严格的监管机构，以及更高的公司披露标准。当你投资于一家在美国上市的公司时，你对 “表外资产” 的信任度更高。在美国以外，你常常会看到经济上毫无意义的分红和回购政策，企业人才通常也会流向美国，因为薪资显著更高。也就是说，我保持开放的态度，因为有一些全球垄断企业，比如 ASML，虽然不在美国上市但提供了良好的价值。你只需要小心在当地法规或你不充分理解的文化差异面前不要成为 “桌上的受害者”。\n\nQ: Why do you maintain such a concentrated portfolio of only 12 to 15 stocks?\n\u003e Q: 你为什么保持如此集中仅 12 到 15 只股票的投资组合？\n\nA: Because moats are qualitative, you need deep, in-depth knowledge to have confidence in your thesis. I want to talk to customers, former employees, and visit trade shows—the \"shoe leather\" work. You cannot do that in a 50-stock portfolio without a massive team, which I don't want. Concentrated is the only way to go for the strategy I run. I've promised my investors we won't dilute that concentration as we grow. Being concentrated is like being pregnant; you either are or you aren't.\n\u003e A: 因为护城河是定性的，你需要深入的知识才能对你的论点充满信心。我想与客户、前员工交谈，并参加行业展会——这些都是 “实地工作”。在没有庞大的团队的情况下，你无法在一个 50 只股票的投资组合中做到这一点，而我并不想要一个庞大的团队。集中是我运营的策略所能采取的唯一方式。我已经向我的投资者承诺，随着我们的增长，我们不会稀释这种集中。\n\nQ: How do you handle the psychological difficulty of selling a position when your thesis was wrong?\n\u003e Q: 当你的论点错误时，你是如何处理卖出头寸的心理难题的？\n\nA: Admitting an error is psychologically hard, but it is necessary. The bulk of our sales have been driven by errors—either the business wasn't as good as we thought, or management wasn't as capable. When the weight of evidence says the thesis is not working out, you have to admit the mistake and move on. Hoping an activist will save your bacon or that the market will turn around is a losing game. It’s better to take a 15% or 20% loss and move that capital into a new idea where you have higher conviction.\n\u003e A: 承认错误在心理上是困难的，但这是必要的。我们的大部分销售是由于错误驱动的——要么是业务没有我们想象的那么好，要么是管理层能力不足。当证据的重担表明论点没有实现时，你必须承认错误并继续前进。希望一位激进分子来拯救你，或者市场会反弹是一场失败的游戏。最好是接受 15% 或 20% 的损失，并将资金转移到一个你有更高信心的新想法中。\n\nQ: What did you mean by the lesson that \"weird isn't wonderful\"?\n\u003e Q: 你所说的 “奇怪不等于美好” 有什么意思？\n\nA: Early on, I thought that as a small manager, I could generate alpha by buying obscure, \"weird\" small-cap businesses that nobody else owned. I realized that sometimes businesses are small and obscure for a reason—they have never succeeded. There is an ego-driven trap in trying to sound \"cool\" by owning things no one else has. I learned that if an amazing mega-cap is staring you in the face at a screamingly cheap valuation, it doesn't matter if everyone knows about it. The goal is to generate returns, not to have a unique portfolio of oddities.\n\u003e A: 在早期，我认为作为一个小管理者，我可以通过购买其他人没有的晦涩 “小型公司” 来产生超额收益。我意识到，有时企业因为某种原因而小而晦涩——它们从未成功过。在试图通过拥有别人没有的事物来显得 “酷” 的过程中，存在一个自我驱动的陷阱。我学到的是，如果一个令人惊叹的大型公司以极便宜的估值摆在你面前，是否人人皆知并不重要。目标是产生回报，而不是拥有独特的奇特投资组合。\n\nQ: How do you approach transparency with your clients, especially when you have to discuss mistakes?\n\u003e Q: 你如何与客户保持透明，特别是当你不得不讨论错误时？\n\nA: You can never be too transparent. Clients have a harder job than I do because they are betting on me as a person, and there is a lot going on in my head they can't see. By being open about my mistakes, I engender a huge amount of trust. I’ve had clients tell me they have to \"calibrate\" their team after I visit because I talk so much about what went wrong that they start to wonder why they invest with me. I have to remind them that every other manager is making the same number of mistakes; they just aren't talking about them. Transparency is a competitive advantage in an industry where it is rarer than it should be.\n\u003e A: 你绝不能显得太透明。客户的工作比我更艰难，因为他们押注于我这个人，而我的思绪中有很多他们看不见的东西。通过对我的错误保持开放，我赢得了大量的信任。我有客户告诉我，在我访问后他们不得不 “调整” 他们的团队，因为我谈论了太多出错的事情，他们开始怀疑为什么会投资于我。我必须提醒他们，其他每位管理者都犯了同样数量的错误；他们只是不在谈论。透明在一个应该比现在更普遍的行业中是一种竞争优势。\n\n# Keywords\n\n|||\n|---|---|\n|Moat|A business concept popularized by Warren Buffett that describes a company's ability to maintain a durable competitive advantage over its rivals.
